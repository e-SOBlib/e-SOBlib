Topic 1: Introduction to Entrepreneurship


1. Introduction
Concept of entrepreneurship:
The word entrepreneur originates from the French word, entreprendre, which means "to undertake" and was first defined by the Irish‚ÄêFrench economist Richard Cantillon. Jean‚Äê Baptiste Say, a French economist, is believed to have coined the word "entrepreneur" in the 19th century. He defined an entrepreneur as an undertaker "one who bears risk and uncertainty in the production and distribution of goods and services.

In simple terms, " An entrepreneur is a person who buys factor services at certain prices with a view to selling its product at uncertain prices" -Richard Cantillon.

Some notable Scholars: who have contributed to the definition of entrepreneurship include-
  • Richard Cantillon (1734): Defined an entrepreneur as someone who buys inputs at certain prices and sells outputs at uncertain prices, emphasizing risk-taking and uncertainty.
  • Joseph Schumpeter (1934): Viewed entrepreneurs as innovators who disrupt existing markets and create new ones, driving economic progress.
  • Jean-Baptiste Say (1803): Described entrepreneurship as shifting economic resources to areas of higher productivity and yield.
  • Peter Drucker (1964): Defined entrepreneurship as searching for change, responding to it, and exploiting opportunities.
In general: Entrepreneurship is the process of identifying opportunities, innovating, organizing resources and taking risks to create value and wealth.
An entrepreneur is an innovative risk-taker who organizes resources to exploit opportunities and create value.



2. Charecteristics of entrepreneurship.
  • Innovation

    Entrepreneurship involves introducing new or improved products, services, or methods of production.

    Example: Mobile money services such as M-Pesa and Tigo Pesa transformed financial transactions.
  • Risk Bearing

    Entrepreneurs take calculated risks due to uncertainty in business outcomes.

    Example: A youth investing capital to start a poultry farming project without guaranteed profits.
  • Opportunity Identification

    Entrepreneurship focuses on identifying unmet needs and market gaps.

    Example: Establishment of water vending businesses in areas facing water shortages.
  • Resource Mobilization

    Entrepreneurship involves organizing and combining factors of production such as land, labor, capital, and technology.

    Example: A farmer using land, hired labor, and microfinance loans to operate an agribusiness.
  • Profit Orientation

    Entrepreneurship is driven by the desire to earn profit as a reward for risk taken.

    Example: Small shop owners expanding their businesses using retained profits.



2. Importance of Entrepreneurship
  • Employment Creation

    Entrepreneurship creates job opportunities and helps reduce unemployment.

    Example: Small manufacturing firms employing local youth.
  • Economic Growth

    Entrepreneurship contributes to national income and industrial development.

    Example: Growth of SMEs contributing to Tanzania‚Äôs GDP.
  • Poverty Reduction

    Entrepreneurship provides income-generating activities and improves living standards.

    Example: Women engaging in tailoring and food vending businesses.
  • Innovation and Technology Development

    Entrepreneurship promotes adoption of modern technology and innovative practices.

    Example: Use of e-commerce platforms to sell agricultural products online.
  • Self-Reliance and Independence

    Entrepreneurship promotes self-employment and reduces dependence on formal employment.

    Example: Graduates starting ICT-based businesses instead of waiting for government jobs.



3. Challenges of Entrepreneurship
  • Lack of Capital

    Many entrepreneurs face difficulties in accessing startup and expansion capital.

    Example: High interest rates charged by financial institutions.
  • Inadequate Skills and Knowledge

    Limited business management and financial skills reduce business performance.

    Example: Poor record keeping among small traders.
  • Market Competition

    Entrepreneurs face stiff competition from established and foreign firms.

    Example: Small retailers competing with supermarkets.
  • Poor Infrastructure

    Inadequate infrastructure increases the cost of doing business.

    Example: Poor road networks affecting transportation of agricultural products.
  • Government Regulations and Taxes

    Complex regulations and multiple taxes discourage business growth.

    Example: Multiple licensing and tax compliance requirements.
